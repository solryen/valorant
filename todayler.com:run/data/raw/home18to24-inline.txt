THINK
Think, Two Steps Hold a cup in front of your child and say clearly, "Pick it up, then give it to me." Keep your hand open and wait through the full pause without repeating the instruction or breaking it into parts. Following a two-part spoken instruction is a meaningful step beyond single commands and shows that your child can hold more than one piece of information at once before acting.
Think, That's Wrong Hold a cup toward your child and say calmly, "That's a spoon." Pause and watch their face for any look of surprise, a headshake, or a correcting sound before you respond at all. Noticing and responding to an incorrect label shows that your child holds the real word in memory and can use it to evaluate what they hear, not only repeat it back.
Think, More or Done? After a simple shared action together—rolling your hands or tapping—stop completely and ask, "More or done?" with a genuinely open expression. Hold still through a full pause and wait for any gesture or sound. Offering a real choice between continuing and stopping gives your child an early and genuine way to direct what happens next in a shared activity.
Think, What Comes Next? Say your child's name and pause, then reach slowly toward them. Do this twice. On the third time, say the name and stop without reaching. Watch whether they lean or reach forward in anticipation. Recognizing that one familiar step predicts another is an early form of sequential thinking and shows your child is building small cause-and-effect expectations in their mind.
Think, Hold On to It Hand your child an empty cup and say, "Hold on to it." Walk three steps away, turn back, and hold your hand out. Say, "Now give it," and wait without stepping closer. Keeping hold of an object through a short delay before responding to a second instruction builds working memory and the ability to stay with a small task across a brief gap in time.
Think, Remember That Hold a cup in front of your child and say its name once clearly. Put it behind your back for a slow count of five, then bring it back out and ask, "What is it?" Wait through a full pause for any word or sound. Recalling a name after a brief delay pushes vocabulary beyond in-the-moment labeling and into early retention, which is a meaningful step forward in language development.
Think, Say It Again Tap your knee once and wait for your child to copy. Then say, "Again," without tapping yourself. Hold still and wait to see if they tap a second time on the word alone. Responding to "again" as a spoken instruction rather than watching an action repeat shows that your child is learning words as tools to direct behavior, not only as names for things.
Think, You First Sit across from your child with a cup between you and say, "You first," with a calm, open expression. Hold still and wait to see what action they choose to start with before following their lead. Handing your child the role of initiator builds confidence and shows them that their actions can begin an interaction, not only follow one.
Think, Fast or Slow Tap your knee quickly twice and say, "Fast." Then tap it twice very slowly and say, "Slow." Do both once more, then tap at one speed and wait with an open, questioning face. Noticing the difference between two speeds of the same action builds attention to rhythm and timing, which are early precursors to pattern recognition and sequential thinking.
Think, Say Something First Hold a cup slightly out of your child's reach and wait in silence with a warm expression. Don't hand it over until any sound or word is attempted, however small. A brief, natural pause before a wanted object creates a real reason to communicate without turning the moment into pressure or a test.
Think, Yes or No? Ask your child a simple question about something they can see—"Is that a bowl?"—while pointing to it. Wait for a nod, a headshake, or any vocal response before you answer yourself. Responding to a real question with a yes-or-no gesture or sound is a clear communicative milestone at this age and shows intentional message-sending rather than just reacting.
Think, In Then Out Hold a cup in one hand and a spoon in the other. Drop the spoon into the cup and say, "In." Tip it out and say, "Out." Do it once more, then hold both items toward your child and wait. Watching a two-part spatial sequence and being invited to continue it combines positional vocabulary with the early understanding that actions can be reversed and repeated.
Think, What Did I Do? Tap your head once with your open hand, pause, and ask, "What did I do?" Wait with your hand completely still. Give your child the full moment to process before tapping again as a cue. Asking a child to recall an action you just performed is a small but real memory retrieval challenge that gives their emerging language a genuine reason to activate.
Think, Tell Me Where Hold a cup and ask, "Where should I put it?" with a curious, open face. Pause and wait. Accept any gesture, a look toward a surface, or a pointing movement as a real answer. Asking your child to direct placement rather than following a command reverses the communication flow and gives them an early experience of guiding another person's action.
Think, Look Then Name Hold a book open toward your child and wait without pointing. Give them five full seconds of free looking, then point to one image and say, "What's that?" Pause through an unhurried wait. Allowing independent looking before asking for a name gives your child the chance to process an image actively, which produces stronger vocabulary engagement than immediate naming does.
Think, Do What I Say Say, "Clap," without clapping yourself. Hold still and wait. Then say, "Stomp," without stomping. Give each instruction a full pause and respond warmly to any attempt at either. Following a spoken action word without a visual model to copy is more demanding than imitation—it asks your child to hold the word's meaning and produce the action from memory alone.
Think, Here or There? Hold a cup and ask, "Here or there?" gesturing toward a spot near you and then toward a spot a few steps away. Wait for your child to point, reach, or shift toward one direction. Choosing between two spatial locations and communicating that choice maps positional words onto real places in the room in a way that invites a clear and visible response.
Think, One More Time Wave once and say, "One more time." Wave again. Then wave once, stop, say, "One more time," and hold still. Wait to see if your child waves or looks expectant before you move. Learning that "one more time" predicts a repeated action builds predictive listening and gives your child a phrase they can soon begin to use to request something they enjoy.
MOVE
Move, Walk Then Run Walk alongside your child at a normal pace and say, "Walk." After a few steps, say, "Run," and pick up your own pace. After a short burst, say, "Walk," again and slow back down. Shifting between two movement speeds on a spoken cue builds body control and begins linking specific words to intentional whole-body changes.
Move, Freeze Walk with your child and say, "Freeze," in a clear, calm voice. Stop your own body completely and wait. After a pause, say, "Go," and start again. Repeat a few times. Stopping a whole-body movement on a specific spoken word builds listening attention and voluntary motor control, two skills that grow steadily together at this age.
Move, Carry It Over There Hand your child a cup and look toward a spot on the far side of the room with a nod. Say, "Take it over there," and walk alongside them without holding their hand. Carrying an object to a distant destination combines walking intention, balance with a held load, and the early ability to hold a small movement goal across several steps.
Move, Jump Together Stand facing your child, take both their hands firmly, and say, "Jump." Spring lightly off the floor yourself while lifting gently through their arms. Repeat two or three times with a clear pause between each. A hand-supported two-foot jump introduces the explosive hip and leg movement of jumping in a safe, connected way and gives your child a first experience of the upward feeling in their body.
Move, Turn Your Head Walk slowly alongside your child and say, "Look that way," while turning your own head to one side as you keep walking. Take a few steps with your head turned before looking forward again. Walking while deliberately shifting visual attention away from the path challenges balance because the direction of movement and the field of vision no longer match, requiring quiet postural adjustment.
Move, Kick It Hold a ball at your child's foot height and let it drop to the floor just in front of their feet. Step back and say, "Kick it," then wait. Retrieve it and offer the same opportunity again. Kicking a stationary ball builds the brief single-leg balance and controlled foot-forward motion that are distinct from walking and steadily developing at this age.
Move, Walk Back Together Stand facing your child, hold both hands loosely, and take three slow steps backward. Stop, turn to stand beside them, and try three backward steps together with one hand held. Walking backward requires trusting footing without visual confirmation, building the proprioceptive awareness and reverse balance that confident toddlers rely on.
Move, Change Direction Walk with your child in one direction, then turn without warning to the right and keep walking. After a few steps, turn left. Let them follow your changes at their own pace. Changing direction unpredictably while walking challenges reactive balance and the spatial awareness needed to follow a path that does not run in a straight line.
Move, Bounce Stand beside your child and bounce on both feet with small, quick pulses—heels barely leaving the floor. Say, "Bounce," do it for a few seconds, then stop and wait. Sustained rhythmic bouncing builds calf and ankle strength, introduces the explosive quality of jumping in a safe range, and trains the bilateral leg timing that full jumps will eventually require.
Move, Carry High, Carry Low Hand your child a cup and say, "Hold it up high." After a few steps, say, "Now hold it low." Walk alongside and alternate between the two heights on your cue. Shifting the height of a carried object while walking demands postural adjustment and core engagement at each transition, turning a simple carrying task into a more physically complex one.
Move, Slow Spin Stand beside your child and slowly turn a full circle on the spot. Stop, plant your feet, and look at your child with a grin. Wait to see if they try any version of a turn. A slow, deliberate self-rotation challenges the vestibular system and trains the body's ability to orient itself after turning, which is a different balance demand than forward movement.
Move, Carry and Turn Hand your child a cup and walk alongside them toward a spot across the room. Halfway there, say, "Turn," and pivot yourself. Watch to see if they turn their body while still holding the cup. Changing direction in the middle of a purposeful carrying task asks your child to coordinate balance, body rotation, and object control all at the same time.
Move, March Fast, March Slow Hold your child's hands lightly and march with exaggerated knee lifts. Say, "Fast march," and quicken the pace. Then say, "Slow march," and drop to a very deliberate lift. Alternate twice. Varying marching tempo within the same movement pattern teaches body-speed awareness and builds the hip and leg endurance needed for sustained active movement.
Move, Crouch and Come Up Crouch low to the floor yourself without sitting and say, "Come down here." When your child squats near you, say, "Now up," and rise back to standing together. Moving from standing to a low squat and back up on a verbal cue builds knee and hip strength and the controlled body awareness that active toddlers use constantly throughout the day.
Move, Walk with Arms Up Hold both arms overhead as you walk beside your child and invite them to do the same. Say, "Arms up," and walk a short distance before lowering them together. Holding both arms raised overhead while walking shifts the center of gravity upward and demands extra core and postural control to stay steady.
Move, One Big Step Stand beside your child and take one exaggerated large step forward, saying, "Big step." Then take a tiny step and say, "Little step." Alternate a few times at a slow, clear pace. Modulating step size intentionally builds motor planning and leg control in a way that makes the difference between large and small movement visible and easy to practice.
Move, Walk to My Voice Move to another part of the room and call your child's name from there. Wait for them to walk toward the sound without any visual cue or gesture from you. Moving purposefully toward a heard voice in a known space builds directional walking confidence and shows your child that locomotion can be guided by sound alone.
Move, Kick and Chase Hold a ball near your child's foot, release it, and let them kick it forward. The moment it moves, say, "Get it," and walk after it alongside them at their own pace. Kicking and immediately chasing a ball trains the quick transition from one motor action to another and builds the spatial tracking of a moving object as a natural extension of movement.
BOND
Bond, Back to Back Sit on the floor back-to-back with your child, pressing gently against each other. Stay still for a moment, breathe quietly, and let them feel your warmth and weight behind them. Physical contact through the back is less intense than face-to-face closeness and gives a child who is active or restless a quiet way to feel connected without being asked to stop and face you.
Bond, Chase and Catch Walk slowly after your child from behind, saying their name in a warm, playful voice. When you get close, wrap them gently in both arms and say, "Gotcha." Let them go and see if they run again. A gentle, predictable chase gives your child the joyful experience of being pursued and caught, teaching them that being found by you is always warm and safe.
Bond, Sing Their Name Make up a simple two-note tune and sing your child's name to it—just the name, nothing more. Repeat it three or four times with the same small melody and wait to see if they smile or echo it. Singing someone's name is a deeply personal form of attention, and the simple repetition makes the moment easy to recognize and genuinely feel as being made entirely for them.
Bond, Almost Hold your hand near your child's tummy without touching, wiggle your fingers slowly, and say, "I'm gonna get you," in a slow, playful voice. Stop just before contact and wait for their reaction. The anticipation of something playful and predictable—without the touch itself—builds joyful social tension and shows your child that shared play can live in the pause before it happens.
Bond, Follow Me Follow You Walk slowly in any direction and glance back at your child. When they follow, stop and wait. Then stand still until they start moving somewhere on their own. When they do, follow them at a gentle distance. Taking turns being the leader and the follower builds the social awareness that interactions have roles, and that your child's choices are worth following with your whole attention.
Bond, Tap and Wait Tap your own chest once and look at your child. Wait. If they tap anywhere on themselves, tap your chest again and nod. Let the exchange run for as many rounds as they will give. A rhythmic touch exchange with a clear pause between turns gives your child a completely non-verbal way to take a real turn, building back-and-forth interaction through the simplest possible shared action.
Bond, Both Hands Open Hold both hands open in front of your child, palms up, and wait. If they place anything in them—or simply touch your palms—close your hands gently around theirs for a moment, then open again. An open hand offered with no request behind it is a quiet physical invitation that puts your child completely in control of what happens next, building their sense of agency within closeness.
Bond, Surprise Face Without any warning, make a sudden large surprised expression—eyebrows up, mouth open—and hold it for three seconds. Then let your face relax completely and wait for your child to react. An unexpected expression in an ordinary moment creates a shared startled-delight response and builds your child's ability to read and respond to clear facial signals quickly.
Bond, Sit in Your Lap Sit on the floor with your legs loose and your hands in your lap. Wait. If your child walks over and settles against you or between your legs, close your arms gently around them and breathe. Receiving your child when they choose to come rather than inviting them teaches them that closeness is always available, and that choosing it themselves is safe and welcomed.
Bond, Our Game Choose one very simple repeated action—a clap, a tap on the knee, a specific sound—and do only that together for a full minute without changing or adding to it. Staying inside one simple shared pattern without escalating it teaches your child that an interaction can be satisfying and complete without growing anywhere, which builds the pleasure of repetition in connection.
Bond, Say Thank You Whenever your child hands you anything at all—even something off the floor—receive it with both hands, pause, and say, "Thank you," looking directly at their face. Being thanked for a small, ordinary act tells your child that their contributions are noticed and valued, deepening the experience of giving as something genuinely worth doing.
Bond, Match My Breath Sit across from your child and breathe in slowly through your nose, then out through your mouth with a slightly audible exhale. Do it two more times and watch to see if their breathing shifts at all. Breathing visibly and slowly in front of your child offers them a real-time model of calm and creates a form of attunement that works at a completely physical, wordless level.
Bond, I See What You See Sit beside your child and look at whatever they are looking at—not narrating, not redirecting, just looking at the same thing for a quiet moment. Then look at their face and back at the thing. Joining your child's gaze without redirecting it communicates that what holds their attention is worth holding yours too, which is one of the quietest forms of genuine respect.
Bond, Slow Walk Together Take your child's hand and walk at a pace slower than either of you would normally go—no destination, no task. Let each step be deliberate and matched exactly to theirs. Walking at an unhurried, shared pace communicates that you are not in a hurry to be anywhere else and that simply moving through the world beside them is exactly enough.
Bond, The Lean While sitting beside your child on the floor, very slowly lean your shoulder into theirs until you are resting gently against each other. Stay there for a quiet moment without talking. A sideways lean into your child's shoulder communicates companionship through the most casual possible physical contact—the kind that happens between people who are simply comfortable being near each other.
Bond, Name the Moment At any point when things feel warm between you—when your child has just laughed or settled or looked at you—say quietly, "This is a good moment," and leave it at that. Naming a shared positive moment as it happens gives your child early language for emotional states that feel good and teaches them that joy is something worth noticing and saying out loud.
Bond, The Long Hold Pick your child up and hold them against your chest without saying anything or starting an activity. Just stand still and hold them for a full thirty seconds. An extended, purposeless hold communicates something that words and activities cannot—that being held by you is a thing in itself, with no other reason needed.
Bond, Find My Eyes Look away from your child—at the wall, the floor, anywhere. Then slowly turn and find their eyes with yours. When you meet their gaze, hold it softly for a moment and then smile. Actively seeking your child's eyes and letting them know you found them is a quiet act of connection that teaches your child their gaze is something worth looking for.





Here are all 54 developmental activities for 19–20 months.
THINK
Think, Not This One Hold a cup in one hand and a spoon in the other. Say, "Give me the spoon," and wait with both hands still. If your child reaches for the cup, keep your expression warm and open and wait a moment longer before trying once more. Selecting one named object from two choices when both are visible builds the kind of precise word understanding that is growing quickly at this age.
Think, That's Silly Hold a spoon against your ear as if it were something else and say, "Hello?" with a playful expression. Then hold it out toward your child and wait to see if they correct you, laugh, or gesture at it. Noticing when an object is being used in the wrong way shows your child is holding real knowledge about what things are for and feeling confident enough to react when something is off.
Think, Ask Me Sit across from your child with a cup in your hand and wait. Say nothing. If they reach for it, hold it still and wait with a warm, expectant face to see if any word or sound comes before you hand it over. A natural, unhurried pause before a wanted object gives your child a real reason to try a word without any pressure attached to the moment.
Think, Do It Again Tap your knee twice and wait for your child to copy. Once they do, say, "Again," without moving yourself. Hold still and see whether they tap a second time on the word alone. Responding to the word "again" rather than watching a repeated action shows that your child is learning words as instructions for their body, not only as names for things around them.
Think, Big Voice, Small Voice Say your child's name in your normal voice. Then lean in and say it in a near-whisper. Pause between the two and watch their face register each one differently. Hearing the same word at two different volumes builds auditory attention and the early awareness that how something is said carries its own kind of meaning.
Think, Show Me the One Hold a book open with two images visible. Say, "Show me the ball," and wait with a still, open expression. Give your child the full moment to locate and point before you respond. Pointing to a named image inside a book rather than a real object nearby is a small but meaningful step toward understanding that pictures represent real things in the world.
Think, Where Did You Put It? Hand your child a spoon and turn away briefly. Turn back and ask, "Where did you put it?" with a genuinely curious face. Let them lead you to wherever they left it. Recalling where they placed an object after a short gap is an early memory task with a real purpose attached, which makes the retrieval feel meaningful rather than like a game with a fixed answer.
Think, Which Is the Big One? Hold a bowl in one hand and a cup in the other at your child's eye level. Say, "Which one is bigger?" and wait. Accept any look, reach, or gesture toward either as a real and complete response. Comparing two familiar objects and making a choice using a size word plants early comparative thinking in a moment that requires no setup and no right answer to feel successful.
Think, Do What I Say Say, "Clap," without clapping yourself. Hold still and wait through a full pause. Then say, "Stomp," without stomping. Respond warmly to any attempt at either word. Following a spoken action word without a movement to copy is more demanding than imitation and shows that your child can hold a word's meaning and act on it from memory.
Think, What Comes After? Build a simple two-part pattern with your child—tap your knee, then tap your shoulder. Do it together twice. Then tap your knee alone and hold still. Wait to see if they add the shoulder tap. Pausing mid-pattern and waiting for your child to complete it shows they are holding the sequence in mind and can predict what should come next.
Think, Tell Me Yes or No Ask your child a question about something they can see right in front of them—"Is this a spoon?"—while holding up the spoon clearly. Wait for a nod, headshake, or any vocal response before answering yourself. Answering a real yes-or-no question intentionally is a clear communicative milestone at this age and shows your child understands that questions expect a specific kind of response back.
Think, Say the Word Point to your own mouth and say, "Ball." Then point slowly to your child's mouth and wait. Look at them with a warm, open face for five full seconds before pointing again. Inviting a word with a gesture and a long wait gives your child the time and the invitation they need to attempt a word on their own terms rather than in response to a demand.
Think, Cup or Bowl? Hold a cup and a bowl toward your child, spaced apart. Say, "Cup," and wait to see which one they look at, reach for, or point to. Offer the same warm pause whether or not they choose correctly. Distinguishing between two familiar containers by name builds precise object vocabulary and the confidence to make a visible choice between two things they know well.
Think, You Do It First Sit across from your child with a cup between you and say, "You first," with a calm, open expression. Hold still and wait to see what action they choose to start with, then follow exactly what they did. Handing your child the role of initiator teaches them that interactions can begin with their action, and that what they choose to do is worth copying and responding to.
Think, After That Roll a ball slowly to your child and wait for them to stop it. Say, "Now give it back," and hold your hands open. Give them the full moment without stepping closer. Completing one action and immediately responding to a direction for the next builds the simple two-step sequencing that is consolidating strongly at this age.
Think, Fast or Slow? Tap your knee quickly and say, "Fast." Then tap it once in a long, slow motion and say, "Slow." Do both once more, then tap at one speed and wait with a raised-eyebrow expression. Noticing and labeling two speeds of the same action builds attention to timing and rhythm, which are among the earliest forms of pattern awareness.
Think, Spoon Goes In Hold a cup and a spoon and say, "Put the spoon in." Hold the cup still and wait without guiding your child's hand. Let them complete the action entirely before you respond. Following a two-word phrase that combines an object and a location builds the kind of practical comprehension that shows language is becoming genuinely useful for your child.
Think, This Is Wrong Tap your head with a spoon and say, "I'm eating," with a puzzled face. Hold still and wait for any sign that your child notices something is wrong—a sound, a look, a correcting gesture. Reacting to mismatched words and actions shows that your child is comparing what they hear against what they know to be true, which is a real and important cognitive leap.
MOVE
Move, Walk and Turn Around Walk with your child in one direction, then say, "Turn around," and pivot yourself clearly. Walk back the same distance and look at your child to see if they follow the full reversal. Reversing direction on a spoken word after a few steps of forward movement adds a planning layer to basic locomotion and builds awareness of the space just traveled.
Move, Carry It Back Hand your child a cup, walk with them to one side of the room, then say, "Bring it back," and return to where you started. Walk alongside them without holding their hand. Carrying an object across a distance and then returning with it builds purposeful locomotion, balance under a light load, and the ability to hold a small movement goal across several steps.
Move, Freeze and Melt Walk with your child and say, "Freeze," in a clear voice. Stop your own body completely and wait. After a pause, say, "Melt," and slowly lower yourself toward the floor. Watch what they do. Adding a second signal—melt—to a freeze game builds more varied body control and gives your child two different kinds of intentional movement responses to practice on a spoken cue.
Move, Step Over My Arm Sit on the floor with one arm stretched out flat. Say, "Step over," and wait for your child to lift their foot over your arm without stepping on it. Offer your hand only if they wobble. Stepping over a visible low obstacle with a deliberate high-knee lift builds hip flexor strength and single-leg balance in a safe, immediate, no-setup way.
Move, Kick with This Foot Hold a ball at your child's right foot and say, "This foot," tapping it gently. Step back and say, "Kick." Then hold it to the left foot and try again. Accept whichever foot moves. Directing attention to one specific foot before kicking builds body awareness and the early ability to move one side of the body with deliberate intention.
Move, Walk Backward with Me Stand facing your child, hold both hands loosely, and say, "Walk backward." Take three slow steps backward yourself. Give their hands just enough contact to feel the direction without pulling. Walking backward requires trusting footing without looking at where the feet are going, building the proprioceptive awareness and reverse balance that growing toddlers are actively developing.
Move, Bounce and Stop Stand beside your child and bounce on both feet in a small, quick rhythm. Say, "Stop," and plant your feet completely still. Wait a moment, then say, "Bounce," and start again. Stopping an active, rhythmic movement on a single word builds voluntary motor control and the attention needed to override a movement that is already underway.
Move, High Step Walk beside your child and exaggerate your own knee lift into a deliberate high step. Say, "High step," and keep a slow, steady pace. Give them time to try it without prompting each foot. Sustained high-knee stepping builds hip flexor strength and bilateral coordination in a way that normal walking pace does not challenge, while remaining completely natural and purposeful.
Move, Throw It Down Hold a ball out toward your child and say, "Throw it down." Guide a gentle downward release if needed on the first attempt, then wait to see if they try the motion on their own for the second turn. A controlled downward release builds wrist and forearm coordination in a different movement plane than a forward throw and is an easier and safer first release for most toddlers this age.
Move, Arms Out, Walk Hold both of your arms out to your sides as you walk beside your child. Say, "Arms out," and keep them wide and steady for several steps before bringing them back down. Walking with arms extended to the sides shifts balance demands onto the core and legs and builds postural awareness in a way that normal arm-at-side walking does not require.
Move, Crouch and Walk Crouch low—not sitting, just bent at the knees—and walk slowly forward in that position for a few steps. Say, "Come down here," and see if your child crouches alongside you. Moving through space in a lowered position demands sustained quadriceps and hip control and gives your child a full-body challenge that differs entirely from upright walking.
Move, Stop at the Wall Walk with your child toward a wall and say, "Stop," just before you reach it. Let them continue to the wall, press both hands against it, and pause before turning back. Stopping near a surface and using it as a physical landmark builds spatial awareness and voluntary movement control in a combined, purposeful way.
Move, Run to Me Stand a few steps away from your child, crouch slightly, open your arms, and say, "Run." Wait for them to move toward you and receive them with both arms as they arrive. Running toward a clear, welcoming destination builds speed confidence, the transition from a walk to a faster gait, and the understanding that running is always safe when it ends in your arms.
Move, Change Your Arms Walk beside your child with your arms hanging naturally. After a few steps, say, "Arms up," and raise both overhead. After a few more, say, "Arms down," and lower them. Alternate twice. Deliberately adjusting arm position while walking in motion builds postural awareness and the ability to manage multiple body parts at once during continuous movement.
Move, Carry High, Put Down Slowly Hand your child a cup and say, "Hold it high." Walk a few steps, then say, "Put it down slowly," and crouch beside them. Watch how they lower it rather than drop it. Transitioning from a raised carry to a controlled placement demands sustained arm control and intentional body lowering that builds precision across a full movement arc.
Move, Kick and Turn Place a ball near your child's foot, say, "Kick," and step back. The moment the ball moves, say, "Turn and get it," and wait for them to pivot and follow it wherever it went. Kicking and immediately pivoting to chase a ball in an unpredictable direction trains the quick transition from a stationary strike to a whole-body pursuit.
Move, Tiptoe Across Stand beside your child, rise on your own toes, and say, "Tiptoe." Walk a few steps in an exaggerated tiptoe before lowering your heels. Let them try the movement at their own level. Rising onto the balls of the feet while moving builds calf and ankle strength and challenges balance in a different plane than flat-footed walking.
Move, March and Stop on the Spot Hold your child's hands lightly and march in place together—knees up, slow and even. After four lifts, say, "Stop," and hold completely still for two seconds before starting the march again. Stopping a rhythmic, bilateral movement at a precise cue builds the connection between a spoken word and a whole-body response, adding a listening layer on top of the physical coordination.
BOND
Bond, My Turn to Follow Walk behind your child at a close, unhurried distance for two full minutes. Go wherever they go, stop when they stop, and turn when they turn. Say nothing unless they look back. Following your child's movement without redirecting it communicates that you find their choices genuinely interesting and that they are capable of leading an interaction from start to finish.
Bond, Almost There Hold your hands near your child's ribs without touching, wiggle your fingers slowly, and say, "I'm coming to get you," in a long, slow playful voice. Stop just before contact and wait. The anticipation of something warm and predictable—without the touch itself—builds joyful social tension and shows your child that shared play can live entirely in the pause before it happens.
Bond, Tap My Knee Sit beside your child and tap your own knee with one finger, once. Look at them and wait. If they tap anything on their own body, tap your knee again and nod. Let the exchange run as long as they will give. A single repeated touch with a clear pause between turns gives your child a completely wordless way to take a real turn and feel the rhythm of a shared back-and-forth.
Bond, Watch Me First Make a slow, clear, silly expression—puff your cheeks out, then let the air go—and hold your child's gaze through the whole thing. Relax your face and wait to see what comes back. Offering your own face as something worth watching invites your child to look carefully at you, build their capacity to read and respond to expressions, and feel the pleasure of shared absurdity.
Bond, Catch You Crouch a few steps away and say, "Come here," with both arms open. When your child walks into them, wrap them gently and say, "Got you," before releasing and doing it once more. A predictable, welcoming physical landing builds walking confidence and tells your child clearly that coming to you is always safe, warm, and worth repeating.

THINK
Think, Which Hand? Hide a spoon in one fist and hold both closed hands out toward your child. Say, "Which hand?" and wait. Open the correct hand after they tap or touch one, with no reaction before they choose. Choosing between two options and committing to that choice is an early problem-solving pattern that builds decision-making and focused attention in a single, immediate gesture.
Think, Say It Before I Do Point to a cup and hold a long pause before naming it. Watch to see if your child attempts the word before you get there. If they do, nod and say it back warmly. If they don't, name it and move on. Leaving space before a familiar label invites your child to retrieve a word they already have, which is a much stronger form of vocabulary practice than simply hearing it named again.
Think, What's It For? Hold a spoon out toward your child and say, "What do we do with this?" in a genuinely curious voice. Wait through a long pause and respond warmly to any gesture, sound, or action they offer. Asking about function rather than name nudges your child one step past labeling toward thinking about purpose, which is an early and important shift in how they understand the objects around them.
Think, One More Roll a ball toward your child once and wait for it to come back. Hold up one finger and say, "One more," then roll it again. After the second roll, keep your hands still and see what they do. Learning that "one more" predicts a repeated action builds predictive listening and gives your child early experience of a phrase they will soon want to use to ask for something themselves.
Think, Louder or Softer Tap a bowl once at normal volume and say, "Loud." Then tap it very softly and say, "Soft." Do it once more, then tap at one level with a questioning expression and wait for any response. Noticing and distinguishing two intensities of the same action builds auditory attention and lays the groundwork for understanding contrast, which will extend well beyond sound into many areas of early thinking.
Think, Wrong Way Hold a cup upside down and try to pretend to drink from its base with an exaggerated confused face. Look at your child and wait to see if they react, reach for it, or offer any correcting gesture. Noticing that an object is being used the wrong way shows your child is holding real knowledge about how things work and feeling confident enough to respond when something doesn't match what they know.
Think, Point First Hold a book open and wait without pointing to anything. Give your child the first move. When they point to any image, name it clearly once, then look back to see if they point to something else. Letting your child lead the pointing before you name anything gives communication a real purpose—they show you something, you respond—which is exactly the back-and-forth language is built on.
Think, Remember the Spoon Show your child a spoon and say its name clearly. Move it slowly behind your back and count aloud to five. Bring it out and ask, "What was it?" Wait through a full unhurried pause. Recalling a name after a brief delay pushes vocabulary beyond in-the-moment labeling into early retention, which is a meaningful step forward in how words begin to stick.
Think, Two Things Together Hold a cup and a spoon and say slowly, "Cup and spoon." Pause. Then set both down and ask, "What did I have?" Wait to see if any word, gesture, or look toward the objects follows. Holding two pieces of information at the same time and retrieving even one of them after a short gap is an early working-memory task that fits squarely within what 20-month-olds are beginning to manage.
Think, Too Big? Hold a spoon over a cup as if trying to fit it in, look puzzled, and say, "Does it fit?" Wait for any response before tipping the spoon in easily. Watch their face shift as it works. A simple mismatch followed by a resolution gives your child a tiny cause-and-effect narrative to process and a reason to form an expectation that something either will or will not work.
Think, Same Hold two cups together and say, "Same." Then hold a cup beside a bowl and say, "Different." Put both cups in front of your child together, look at them with a curious expression, and wait. Introducing the idea that two things can match each other is one of the earliest classification concepts and gives your child a new kind of thinking tool beyond naming individual objects.
Think, I'll Wait for a Word Hold a ball just out of your child's reach, look at them warmly, and wait in complete silence. Keep your expression open and patient. Do not hand it over until any sound or word attempt is made. A natural, unhurried pause before a wanted object creates a real reason to communicate and is one of the most effective ways to invite early word attempts without any pressure attached to the moment.
Think, Clap Once or Twice Clap once and say, "One." Then clap twice and say, "Two." Do it again slowly, then clap once and hold still with a raised-eyebrow look. Wait to see if they respond to the number or the count. Pairing a spoken number with a matching action gives your child a first concrete experience of one and two as distinct and meaningful quantities before any formal counting concept is needed.
Think, Where Did It Go? Hold a spoon clearly in front of your child, then move your hand slowly behind your back. Say, "Where did it go?" and wait. Give a long pause before bringing it back out. Holding the memory of an object and actively waiting for its return shows that object permanence is now well established and that your child can hold an expectation comfortably across a short gap.
Think, Tell Me No Hold up a cup and say, "Is this a ball?" with a slightly puzzled face. Wait to see if your child shakes their head, says no, or offers any correcting sound. Receive whatever comes back warmly. Recognizing and expressing that something is wrong—not just naming what is right—is an important cognitive and communicative step that is consolidating clearly in the months around this age.
Think, Big One First Hold a bowl in one hand and a cup in the other and say, "Give me the big one." Hold both still with an open expression. Wait for your child to look, reach, or gesture toward one without any additional hint. Selecting an object based on a size description rather than a name builds the early comparative thinking that is just beginning to become reliable in the second half of the second year.
Think, After You Sit Say, "Sit down," and wait. Once your child sits, say, "Now stand up," and wait again. Keep a warm, unhurried tone with a full pause between each direction. Following two sequential single-step directions one after the other—not simultaneously—is a skill consolidating clearly around this age and builds both listening comprehension and intentional body awareness.
Think, You Choose Place a cup on one side and a spoon on the other, both in plain view. Say, "You choose," with a warm, genuinely open expression. Wait without pointing or hinting toward either one. Being given a real choice with no guidance builds your child's sense of agency and shows them that their preferences are visible, valid, and interesting to you.
MOVE
Move, Walk and Freeze Walk alongside your child at a comfortable pace and say, "Freeze," in a clear, calm voice. Stop your own body completely and wait. After a pause, say, "Go," and start again. Stopping a whole-body movement on a specific spoken word builds voluntary motor control and the listening attention that grows steadily when movement and language are connected.
Move, Run and Stop Walk with your child, then say, "Run," and pick up your pace briefly. After a short burst say, "Stop," and plant your feet. Repeat the full run-and-stop cycle two or three times. Transitioning between two movement speeds and stopping on a word builds body control and begins linking specific spoken cues to intentional physical changes in the whole body.
Move, Turn and Come Back Walk four or five steps with your child, then say, "Turn around," and pivot yourself clearly. Walk back the same distance and stop. Let your child follow the full sequence at their own pace. Reversing direction on a verbal cue after a few steps of forward movement adds a small planning element to basic locomotion and builds awareness of the space behind them.
Move, Big Step, Little Step Take one exaggerated large step forward and say, "Big step." Then take the tiniest possible step and say, "Little step." Alternate a few times slowly and clearly, then look at your child's feet. Modulating step size with intention builds motor planning and leg control in a way that makes the physical difference between large and small movement visible and immediately practical to copy.
Move, Carry and Turn Hand your child a cup and walk alongside them. Halfway to any point, say, "Turn," and pivot yourself. Watch to see if they turn their whole body while keeping hold of the cup. Changing direction in the middle of a purposeful carrying task asks your child to coordinate balance, body rotation, and object control simultaneously, which is more demanding than any one of those skills alone.
Move, Stomp and Walk Take three loud stomping steps forward, then drop back to a quiet, normal walk. Say, "Stomp," then, "Walk," as you shift between them. Let your child follow the pattern at their own level. Alternating between forceful and controlled foot placement builds leg and ankle strength, deliberate body awareness, and the ability to modulate intensity within the same repeated movement.
Move, Arms Up, Walk Across Say, "Arms up," and raise both of your arms overhead as you walk slowly forward. After several steps, say, "Arms down," and lower them. Walk beside your child throughout. Walking with arms raised overhead shifts the center of gravity upward and demands extra core and postural control to stay balanced, making a simple walk into a genuine coordination challenge.
Move, Kick It Far Place a ball just in front of your child's foot and step back. Say, "Kick it far," and wait. After they kick it, walk alongside them to wherever the ball landed without racing ahead. A goal-directed kick that invites distance combines the brief single-leg weight shift of kicking with the purposeful locomotion of chasing, training the quick transition between two motor actions.
Move, Tiptoe Over There Stand beside your child, rise on your own toes, and say, "Tiptoe." Walk a few slow steps forward and look back. Accept any heel-rising at all as a full and complete attempt. Rising onto the balls of the feet while walking builds calf and ankle strength and challenges balance in a different plane than flat-footed walking, which toddlers find genuinely interesting to try.
Move, Throw and Turn Around Help your child hold a ball and guide a forward release. The moment the ball leaves their hands, say, "Turn around," and wait for them to pivot and locate where it went. Releasing an object and immediately reorienting toward its path builds spatial tracking and the early body awareness that one action can have a consequence that requires a whole new movement response.
Move, Crouch and Walk Crouch low—knees bent, not sitting—and walk slowly forward for a few steps. Say, "Come down here," and move beside your child so they can see the height you are holding. Moving through space in a lowered position demands sustained knee and hip control and gives your child a full-body challenge that differs entirely from upright walking in its demands.
Move, March and Clap March in place with your child with exaggerated knee lifts. After a few beats, add one clap overhead at the top of each lift. Say, "March and clap," and keep the rhythm slow and even. Coordinating arms and legs in a deliberate rhythm builds bilateral coordination and the ability to manage two movement patterns at the same time, which is a genuine step forward in motor organization.
Move, Kick with the Other Foot After your child kicks a ball once, hold it back at their other foot and tap it gently. Say, "This one now," and step back. Accept any foot movement toward the ball as a real and complete attempt. Deliberately using the non-dominant foot builds bilateral motor development and body-side awareness, challenging your child to produce a familiar movement from an unfamiliar starting point.
Move, Walk Through the Gap Stand a few feet apart from a wall with clear open space between you and it. Call your child through the gap with an open expression and wait on the other side for them to navigate it alone. Walking through a visible but bounded space requires spatial judgment and controlled foot placement that challenges coordination beyond open-floor walking in a simple and immediate way.
Move, Bounce and Freeze Stand beside your child and bounce on both feet in a small, quick rhythm. Say, "Freeze," and stop completely still mid-bounce. Hold it for two seconds, then say, "Bounce," and resume. Stopping a rhythmic, active movement at a precise cue builds the connection between a spoken word and a whole-body response and adds a listening layer on top of the physical coordination.
Move, One Foot Up Hold both of your child's hands loosely and say, "One foot up." Lift your own foot slightly and hold it for two seconds. Lower it, switch sides, and try the other. Let them lead how long they hold. Briefly supporting all weight on one leg builds the single-leg stance control that underlies every step of walking and is actively maturing throughout the early toddler years.
Move, Walk to My Voice Move to another part of the room and say your child's name from there. Wait for them to walk toward the sound without a visual cue or any guiding gesture from you. Moving purposefully toward a heard voice in a known space builds directional walking confidence and shows your child that locomotion can be guided by something they hear rather than something they see.
Move, Slow and Fast Hands Hold both arms out and wave them slowly, saying, "Slow." Then wave them quickly and say, "Fast." Do it once more, then hold your arms out with a questioning look and wait. Noticing and producing two speeds of the same arm movement builds attention to timing in the body and gives your child a concrete experience of the same action feeling very different at different speeds.

Anticipating the end of a familiar phrase and filling it in is an early language milestone that shows your child is holding sentence structure in memory and using it to predict what comes next.

Think, What Did I Change? Hold a cup and a spoon in front of your child for a moment. Turn away briefly, swap them to opposite hands, and turn back. Tracking a small change in a familiar scene after a very short gap builds visual memory and the habit of comparing what is in front of them now against what they remember from a moment ago.
Think, How Many Taps? Tap the floor once and say, "One." Tap twice and say, "Two." Then tap once without counting aloud and hold your hand still with a questioning look. Wait through the full pause before responding to whatever they choose. Selecting an object by size description rather than name requires your child to hold a comparative word in mind and apply it to two real objects at once, which is a concrete step beyond basic labeling.

Accept any gesture, a sound, or an imitation of either part as a real attempt. Wait for a nod, a headshake, or any gesture of response. Judging whether two objects have a functional relationship is a step toward early classification thinking and shows that your child is building knowledge about how objects belong to each other in the world.

Think, Say It Two Ways When your child uses a single word, nod warmly and then say the same idea back in a short two-word phrase. Pause, then look at them with an open expression and wait. Hearing their own message expanded by one word gives your child a natural model of the next language step without any pressure to repeat or correct anything they said.
Think, What's the Same? Hold two spoons side by side and say, "What's the same?" with a genuinely curious look. Give your child a long pause to look, touch, or respond in any way before you tap them together. Looking for what two objects share rather than how they differ is a different kind of comparative thinking and builds the habit of finding connection between things rather than only contrast.
Think, One Too Many Hold out two cups and one spoon and say, "Which one doesn't belong?" with a puzzled face. Hold all three still and wait. Accept any reach, look, or gesture toward any of the three as a real attempt. Spotting the odd one out from a simple grouping requires holding a matching rule in mind and checking each object against it, which is an early and accessible form of categorical thinking.
Think, What Did I Say? Say a single clear familiar word—"blanket"—then count silently to ten before asking, "What did I say?" Keep your face warm and open through the full wait before responding to whatever comes back. Holding one spoken word in mind across a brief silent gap is a small but genuine short-term memory challenge that gives vocabulary practice a real retrieval demand.
Think, Ask Me Again When your child asks for something with a gesture or a word, pause, smile, and say, "Ask me again," warmly before handing it over. Give the full moment without any hurry. Repeating a communicative attempt on request builds intentional, deliberate language use and teaches your child that communication is something they actively control rather than something that just happens.
Think, Before or After? Clap once and then tap your knee once. Ask, "What did I do first?" Hold still and wait. Accept any gesture, sound, or imitation of either action as a genuine and complete answer. Recalling the order of two actions that just happened builds sequential memory and gives your child early language for the idea that events have a position in time relative to each other.
Think, Fix It Hold a cup upside down and say, "Something's wrong," with a puzzled expression. Hand it toward your child and wait to see if they flip it right side up or gesture toward the correction. Recognizing that an object is in the wrong orientation and acting to fix it shows that your child holds confident knowledge about how things work and feels capable of setting something right.
Think, You Tell Me No Hold up a ball and say, "Is this a spoon?" with an exaggerated questioning face. Wait for a headshake, a "no," or any correcting sound before responding to whatever comes back. Knowing when to say no to a wrong label shows your child is holding the real word firmly enough in memory to use it as a standard for checking what they hear.
Think, How Many Are There? Hold up two fingers clearly and say, "How many?" Wait through a full unhurried pause. Then hold up one finger and ask again. Accept any number sound, gesture, or look as a complete response. Connecting a visible quantity to a spoken number gives your child a concrete experience of number as something that describes the world rather than only a sequence to recite.
Think, Your Rule Do a simple repeated action—tap your knee once—and then look at your child and say, "Now you make a rule," with a genuinely open expression. Stay still and wait to see what they choose to offer. Inviting your child to decide what comes next in a structured moment builds their experience of agency within a pattern and gives language and gesture a real purpose for directing shared activity.
Think, What's It For? Hold a bowl out and say, "What do we do with this?" in a curious voice. Wait through a long pause and respond warmly to any gesture, word, or mimed action they offer in return. Asking about function rather than name nudges your child past labeling toward thinking about purpose, which is an early but important shift in how they understand the objects around them.
Think, Almost Right Say a short simple phrase with one word slightly off—"You sit on a spoon"—with a confused face. Pause and wait to see whether your child laughs, shakes their head, or offers a correction. Catching a word that doesn't fit a familiar phrase requires holding knowledge about both the words and the sense of the sentence, which is a genuine early step in metalinguistic awareness.
Think, Show Me Two Hold up two fingers and say, "Give me two taps." Hold still and wait. Then hold up one finger and say, "Give me one tap." Keep your expression open and respond warmly to any attempt at either. Connecting a visual number cue to a matching physical count gives your child a first concrete experience of one and two as distinct quantities that can be seen, heard, and produced.
Think, I'll Wait for More When your child says a single word toward you, nod warmly and stay completely still. Say, "Can you tell me more?" and wait a full patient beat before responding to whatever follows or doesn't. Holding space for a second word without pressure gives your child a natural opportunity to practice combining words, which is the central language step of the months surrounding two years old.
Think, Find It Without Looking Tell your child to find the cup without letting them see you hold it behind your back first. Say, "It's somewhere near me," and wait while they search. Keep your expression warm and give it when they find it. Searching for an object using only a spoken spatial clue rather than a visual one asks your child to hold a verbal description in mind and use it to guide a real action in the world.
MOVE
Move, Run and Come Back Take a few quick steps away from your child and pause. Look back with an open expression and wait for them to run toward you. When they arrive, crouch and open your arms to receive them warmly. Running toward a clear and welcoming destination builds speed confidence, the transition from a walk to a faster gait, and the understanding that running toward you always ends in something good.
Move, Kick and Tell Me Where Place a ball near your child's foot, say, "Kick it," and step back. After it moves, ask, "Where did it go?" before either of you walks after it. Wait for any point, look, or word in the ball's direction. Pausing after a kick to name where the ball went gives a physical action a language layer and builds spatial vocabulary in a moment when the meaning of the words is immediate and visible.
Move, Jump Over My Leg Sit on the floor with one leg stretched out flat and say, "Jump over," while pointing to your leg. Stay completely still and offer one hand only if they wobble on their first attempt. Jumping over a clear, low, stable obstacle with a deliberate two-foot lift builds explosive leg strength, timing, and the early spatial judgment of clearing something on the ground.
Move, March and Stop on the Word March in place together with deliberate knee lifts. Call out "Stop," at an unexpected moment and freeze every part of your body. Hold for three seconds, then say, "March," and resume. Stopping a rhythmic bilateral movement at an unpredictable cue builds the listening attention and voluntary motor control that grow steadily when movement and language are connected in live play.
Move, Walk Backward, Count the Steps Hold your child's hands loosely and walk three slow steps backward together while counting aloud, "One, two, three." Stop, then try four steps. Let the number grow as they feel more confident. Walking backward with a spoken count attaches a language element to a balance challenge and gives your child a way to track and anticipate the end of a movement that can't be visually confirmed.
Move, Throw High, Say Up Hold a ball out toward your child and say, "Throw it up." As they release it, say, "Up," in a rising voice to match the arc. Retrieve it and wait to see if they reach out for another turn. A gentle upward release is a different motor pattern from a forward throw and builds shoulder rotation and the early spatial awareness of an object moving in a vertical arc away from the body.
Move, Spin and Find Me Stand a short distance away from your child and say, "Spin around, then find me." Step to a new spot while they turn. Wait for them to complete the spin and locate you before you move again. Completing a self-rotation and reorienting to find a specific person builds vestibular processing and the spatial awareness needed to reorganize body position after turning through space.
Move, Stomp a Pattern Stomp twice, pause, then stomp once. Say, "Two, then one." Do it again and this time stop after the two stomps. Wait to see if your child adds the single stomp to complete the pattern. Following a rhythmic physical sequence and completing its last beat builds pattern memory, timing, and the bilateral coordination that connects deliberate movement to anticipated structure.
Move, Tiptoe and Freeze Rise on your toes and say, "Tiptoe," while walking slowly forward. After a few steps, say, "Freeze," and hold the tiptoe position without lowering your heels. Wait two seconds, then walk on. Holding a balance-demanding position on a spoken cue adds a double challenge—sustaining an unstable stance and responding to a word at the same time—that reflects the growing motor control of this age.
Move, Carry and Turn Around Hand your child a cup and walk alongside them across the room. Say, "Turn around," partway across and pivot yourself clearly. Walk back to the start together without holding their hand. Changing direction in the middle of a carrying task asks your child to coordinate balance, body rotation, and object control simultaneously, which is more demanding than any one of those in isolation.
Move, Kick with Each Foot Place a ball in front of your child and say, "Kick it." After the first kick, retrieve the ball, hold it near their other foot, tap that foot gently, and say, "This one now." Step back and wait. Deliberately using the non-dominant foot builds bilateral motor development and body-side awareness, asking your child to produce a familiar movement from an unfamiliar and slightly awkward starting point.
Move, Big Step Over Stand beside your child and take one exaggerated high step over an imaginary line, saying, "Big step over." Look at them, then do it once more. Wait to see what size step they produce on their own. An exaggerated high-knee step over a self-imagined obstacle builds hip flexor strength, single-leg balance, and the motor planning to lift the foot intentionally higher than walking normally requires.
Move, Run, Stop, Sit Down Walk with your child, say, "Run," and pick up pace briefly. Then say, "Stop," and freeze. Then say, "Sit down," and lower yourself to the floor. Pause at each cue before moving to the next. Responding to three distinct movement words in a fixed, clear sequence builds sequential listening and the body control needed to shift deliberately between three different whole-body states.
Move, Throw at the Wall and Catch It Back Stand beside your child near the wall and help them throw a ball so it hits and returns. Step to the side and wait to see what they do when it bounces back toward them. Throwing at a surface that sends the ball back introduces physical cause and effect in space and gives your child an immediate and unpredictable reason to produce a second, different movement response.
Move, Walk the Slow Way, Walk the Fast Way Walk beside your child at the slowest pace either of you can manage for a few steps, then say, "Now fast," and pick up to a brisk walk. Say, "Slow again," and return. Keep both transitions clean and clear. Shifting between two deliberate walking speeds on a spoken word builds body awareness and the intentional control of forward momentum in a way that a single steady pace never requires.
Move, Balance and Count Hold your child's hand and say, "One foot up." Lift your own foot and count aloud with them—"One, two, three"—before lowering it. Try the other side and aim for the same count or longer. Holding a single-leg balance while counting aloud layers a language task on top of a physical one and gives your child a way to track and extend their own effort with a shared, audible measure.
Move, Change Direction on the Clap Walk together across the room and clap once without warning. On the clap, pivot together and walk the other direction. Clap again to switch back. Keep the timing unpredictable. Changing direction on a non-verbal auditory cue rather than a spoken word builds reactive balance and the spatial awareness needed to shift course quickly in response to something heard rather than said.
Move, Crouch Walk Crouch low—knees bent, not sitting—and walk slowly forward for several steps while staying in that lowered position. Say, "Can you walk like this?" and let your child find their own version. Moving through space in a sustained lowered position demands continuous knee and hip control and gives your child a full-body challenge that is genuinely distinct from upright walking in its physical demands.
BOND
Bond, Morning Check First thing in the morning, hold your child and do a slow, warm check—"How are your hands? How's your tummy? Is your nose okay?"—like you are genuinely finding out. Beginning the day with named, attentive physical contact tells your child that being fully seen comes before anything else and builds a felt sense of safety that carries through the hours ahead.
Bond, Your Story After any small moment in the day, crouch to your child's level and say, "Tell me what happened," with a calm, genuinely interested face. Wait through whatever words, sounds, or gestures come back. Inviting your child to narrate an experience rather than rushing past it builds early storytelling, gives language a real and immediate purpose, and tells them clearly that their version of events is worth hearing.
Bond, I'll Follow You Walk quietly behind your child wherever they go for two full minutes. Match their pace, turn when they turn, and stop when they stop. Say nothing unless they look back at you first. Following your child's movement without redirecting it communicates that you find their choices genuinely interesting and that they are fully capable of leading something from beginning to end.
Bond, Slow Chase Walk after your child slowly, saying their name in a long, warm, playful voice. When you get close, wrap both arms around them gently and say, "Got you." Release and wait to see if they go again. A gentle, predictable chase gives your child the experience of being pursued and caught and teaches them that being found by you is always warm, safe, and worth repeating as many times as they want.
Bond, Two Voices Start humming a single steady note and let your child hear it. When they make any sound at all, shift your note toward theirs and stay with the match for as long as they hold it. Adjusting your voice to follow your child's rather than offering a model for them to copy is a direct act of attunement and teaches them that their voice shapes what you do, which builds real confidence in expression.
Bond, Name the Feeling When your child makes a clear expression—pleased, frustrated, surprised—pause and name it quietly and accurately. Say it warmly and without any judgment, then leave space for what comes next. Hearing a calm, accurate word placed on a feeling in real time builds your child's inner emotional vocabulary and teaches them that the people who love them can read and reflect what they feel.
Bond, Your Idea Sit across from your child with nothing between you and say, "You decide what we do," with a genuinely open expression. Stay still and wait. Accept any movement, sound, or gesture as a real decision. Giving your child complete authority over what happens next in a shared moment builds their experience of agency within connection and tells them that their initiative is always worth following.
Bond, The Long Hold Pick your child up and hold them against your chest without saying anything or starting any activity. Stand still and hold for a full thirty seconds before setting them down. An extended, purposeless hold communicates something that words and activities cannot—that being held by you is a complete thing in itself, with no other reason needed for it to happen.
Bond, Sit Together, Say Nothing Sit side by side on the floor and be near each other for a minute with no activity and no words. Just companionable closeness with no direction and nothing expected from either of you. A moment of easy, shared stillness with no goal attached builds your child's experience of being comfortable beside you, which is one of the quieter but most lasting forms of connection there is.
Bond, Say One True Thing At any quiet moment, look at your child and say one specific, observed thing you genuinely noticed—"You kept trying that"—and then let it rest completely without adding more. A single specific statement lands more deeply than general praise and teaches your child that you are paying close attention to who they actually are rather than how they are performing for you.
Bond, Walk Away, Come Back Walk a short distance away from your child slowly, pause with your back to them for a moment, then turn and walk back. Crouch and open your arms when you return. A gentle, predictable departure and warm return is one of the most direct ways to reinforce the sense that you always come back, which is the quiet foundation of secure attachment at every age.
Bond, I Heard You Whenever your child makes any sound toward you—a word, a point, a look with a sound behind it—pause completely, face them fully, and say, "I heard you," before anything else. Acknowledging a communication attempt before responding to its content teaches your child that their voice reliably produces your full attention, which is the most powerful possible reason to keep using it.
Bond, Here I Am From across the room, catch your child's eye and raise one hand in a small, quiet wave. Hold it for a moment and lower it slowly. Do nothing else and wait to see what they do. A small, unprompted acknowledgment across a distance tells your child that you are aware of them even when you are not beside them, which is a quiet and deeply reliable form of felt security.
Bond, I See What You See Sit beside your child and look at whatever they are looking at—not narrating, not redirecting—just looking at the same thing quietly for a moment. Then look at their face and back at the thing. Joining your child's gaze without comment communicates that what holds their attention is worth holding yours too, which is one of the quietest and most genuine forms of respect one person can offer another.
Bond, Good Night, You At the end of the day, hold your child against your chest, say their name once very softly, and breathe out slowly. Stay still for one full breath before setting them down for the night. A brief, consistent closing moment that is entirely theirs—just their name and your steady breath—gives your child a daily felt signal that the day ended safely and that you were with them all the way through it.
Bond, The Waiting Hug Stand with your arms open at your child's level and stay completely still. Do not call their name or prompt them. Wait for them to choose to walk in on their own terms entirely. Offering closeness without asking for it teaches your child that a hug is always available and that choosing it themselves is something you quietly welcome every single time it happens.
Bond, What Should We Do? At a natural pause in your day, lean toward your child and say quietly, "What should we do?" Wait through their sounds, gestures, or silences and respond to whatever comes as though it was a full and real answer. Treating your child as someone whose input shapes what happens next builds their sense of agency within the relationship and opens the door to real dialogue well before full sentences are consistently available.
Bond, End It Together When any part of the day finishes, stop, look at your child, and say, "All done," while spreading both hands open slowly. Pause and let the moment close quietly before moving on to what comes next. A consistent closing phrase and matching gesture gives your child a reliable, calm signal that a transition is coming, which builds trust in the shape of the day and reduces the friction that endings can bring.
